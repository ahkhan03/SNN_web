## A toy that explains the whole thing

Imagine a single point $x$ living in a flat 2-D plane. Two things act on it:

1. **A pull.** There's a target $x^\star$ somewhere, and our point feels a force pulling it toward $x^\star$ at every moment. That force is the **gradient** of an objective — let's say $f(x) = \tfrac{1}{2}\|x - x^\star\|^2$ — and the point moves in the direction that decreases $f$.

2. **A wall.** There's a line (or two) that the point can't cross. Each wall is an **inequality constraint**: $c_i^\top x + d_i \le 0$.

If there were no walls, the point would just glide to $x^\star$ — that's plain gradient descent. The interesting question is what happens when the gradient pulls the point *into* a wall.

## The spike

Here's the move. Between collisions, the point drifts by a small step in the gradient direction. Just before it would cross a wall, a **spike** fires: a discrete correction that pushes the point back to the wall, exactly along the wall's normal, by exactly the amount needed to stay on the feasible side.

That's it. That's the whole algorithm: drift, spike, drift, spike. The drifts shrink the objective; the spikes preserve feasibility. Eventually the two balance, and the point sits at the constrained optimum.

## Why call it *spiking*?

A leaky integrate-and-fire (LIF) neuron has a very simple dynamic: its voltage $V_i$ drifts according to its inputs, and when $V_i$ crosses a threshold $T_i$, it **fires a spike** and resets. Repeat.

If you write down those dynamics for a population of $n$ LIF neurons with the right recurrent connectivity, you get exactly the system above:

$$
\dot x \;=\; -\nabla f(x) \;-\; C^\top s(t)
$$

where $s(t)$ is a sum of delta functions — the spike train. The threshold-crossings of the LIF model and the constraint-violations of the optimizer are *the same event*, viewed from two angles. Each spike is a primal-dual update.

This isn't a metaphor. It's a literal equivalence first formalized by Mancoo, Boerlin and Machens at NeurIPS 2020, and the [`snn_opt`](https://github.com/ahkhan03/SNN_opt) library is a clean Python implementation of the discretized version.

## What does this buy us?

Three useful things:

- **A new way to think about classical solvers.** Many things you already know — projected gradient methods, primal-dual algorithms, active-set methods — fall out as discretizations of this single dynamic. The spike raster is a diagnostic that doesn't exist in their classical form.
- **Hardware naturalness.** The primitives are matrix-vector multiplies, comparisons, and resets. Those are exactly the operations digital neuromorphic chips are good at (Loihi, SpiNNaker, TrueNorth).
- **A unifying lens for ML.** A surprising number of classical machine-learning problems — SVM duals, ridge regression, PCA, kernel ridge regression, collaborative filtering, portfolio optimization — can be cast as constrained QPs and solved by exactly the same dynamics.
